Executive Track / Security Fluency

From founder instinct to board‑level command

A stage-by-stage path for a CEO and COO to build real security fluency as the company grows — not to become engineers, but to make good calls, ask the right questions, and speak the customer's language.

01
Security Literacy
Pre‑seed / Seed
~1–10 people · no dedicated security hire yet
Learn
  • Core vocabulary: CIA triad, threat vs. vulnerability vs. risk
  • The threat landscape for your own product category
  • NIST CSF at a glance — the 5 functions, not the detail
  • What "shared responsibility" means with your cloud provider
Do
  • Turn on MFA everywhere, enforce a password manager company-wide
  • Write a one-page incident response plan — who calls whom
  • Sit in on your first pen test or security review, even silently
Course / certification ladder
Credential
ISC2 Certified in Cybersecurity (CC) — free exam, entry-level vocabulary and frameworks. Good first cert for a non-technical exec, ~2 weeks of self-study.
Hands-on
TCM Security — Practical Ethical Hacking (PEH) — optional, if you want to actually see how an attack works instead of just reading about it. Self-paced, ~20–25 hrs, no prerequisites.
Signal you're ready to move on: you can explain your own product's threat model in two minutes, unaided.
02
Operational Fluency
Series A
first security/compliance hire · first enterprise customers
Learn
  • SOC 2 and ISO 27001 — what auditors actually check, and why customers ask
  • Vendor & third-party risk basics (you're now both vendor and buyer)
  • How to read a penetration test report and a risk register
  • Data classification: what "sensitive" means for your customers' data
Do
  • Run your first tabletop incident-response exercise, COO facilitates
  • Build a security answers doc for sales — stop reinventing it per deal
  • Set a cadence: monthly 30-min security review with the exec team
Course / certification ladder
Credential
CompTIA Security+ — the standard baseline cert for anyone touching security decisions. Signals fluency to auditors and enterprise customers alike.
Hands-on
TCM Security — Practical Junior Penetration Tester (PJPT) — a step up from PEH, builds on it. Worth it if you or the COO want direct credibility when reviewing pen test findings, not just a summary from the vendor.
Credential
ISO/IEC 27001 Foundation — 1–2 day course, gives you the audit's actual structure before your first certification cycle.
⚠ Don't advance until: you can walk a customer's security questionnaire without handing it entirely to engineering.
03
Strategic Command
Growth stage
security team of its own · regulated or enterprise customers
Learn
  • Regulatory landscape relevant to your customers: GDPR, CCPA, HIPAA, PCI-DSS
  • Zero-trust architecture, at the concept level — why it changes buying decisions
  • Cyber insurance: what's covered, what voids a claim
  • Security due diligence as it applies to fundraising and M&A
Do
  • Own the security narrative in the data room for your next raise
  • Bring the CISO/security lead into board prep, not just the demo
  • Benchmark your posture against 2–3 competitors' public trust pages
Course / certification ladder
Hands-on
TCM Security — Practical Network Penetration Tester (PNPT) — the credential-grade cert in their line, full network compromise + reporting. Optional at this stage, but strong if either of you wants a genuine practitioner-level credential rather than an executive-briefing level of knowledge.
Credential
CISM or CRISC (ISACA) — built for people who manage security risk and programs rather than configure firewalls. Closer to your actual day job than CISSP is.
Credential
ISO/IEC 27001 Lead Implementer — useful once you're the one deciding how the ISMS gets built, not just reading the audit output.
Signal you're ready to move on: a regulator or enterprise auditor could ask you a direct question and get a straight answer, not a deflection to engineering.
04
Executive Mastery
Scale / Pre‑IPO
board reporting on cyber risk is routine · public scrutiny is real
Learn
  • Board-level cyber risk governance — what a director should be asking you
  • Breach disclosure law: SEC 8-K rules, state notification timelines
  • Crisis communications for a live incident — internal, customer, press, all at once
  • Executive programs worth the time: SANS "Security Leadership" tracks, Harvard/MIT exec cyber programs
Do
  • Run a full-scale breach simulation with legal, comms, and the board observing
  • Present cyber risk as a standing board agenda item, quarterly
  • Represent the company publicly on security — panels, customer advisory boards
Course / certification ladder
Credential
NACD Cyber-Risk Oversight Certificate — built specifically for boards and C-suite, the closest thing to a required credential at this stage.
Credential
CISSP (concepts, not necessarily the exam) — the common language your CISO and every enterprise auditor already speaks; worth knowing even secondhand.
Hands-on
SANS MGT/LEG-series executive courses (e.g. cyber law, security leadership) — short, expensive, built for exactly this seat.
The bar here: you could brief the board mid-incident, live, without a script — and they'd trust the read.

The habit loop — running at every stage in parallel

Weekly15-minute threat brief from your security lead — headlines, not detail
MonthlyExec-team security review — open items, incidents, near-misses
QuarterlyTabletop exercise, rotating scenarios — ransomware, insider, vendor breach
YearlyOne conference or peer network touchpoint — RSA, Black Hat, a local CISO roundtable
Roadmap calibrated for a cybersecurity-sector startup — earlier and deeper security fluency is expected of leadership than in most other verticals, since it's the product, not just the plumbing.
